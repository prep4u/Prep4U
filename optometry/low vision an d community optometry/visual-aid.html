Low vision is typically defined as:

  • Visual acuity worse than 6/18 but better than light perception, even with the best correction.
  • Severe visual field loss, such as tunnel vision or central scotomas (blind spots).

Common causes of low vision include:

  • Age-related macular degeneration (ARMD)
  • Glaucoma
  • Diabetic retinopathy
  • Retinitis pigmentosa
  • Cataracts (if inoperable)
  • Optic nerve atrophy
  • Congenital or developmental conditions

While the damage may be irreversible, people with low vision can learn to use their remaining vision effectively‚Äîwith the right tools and training.

Visual aids are only part of the picture. Rehabilitation helps individuals adapt and thrive despite vision loss. This often involves a multidisciplinary team, including optometrists, occupational therapists, mobility instructors, and psychologists.

Key Components of Low Vision Rehabilitation

  • Assessment of visual function: Beyond acuity‚Äîincludes contrast sensitivity, glare sensitivity, and visual fields.
  • Goal-oriented training: Tailored plans for personal goals (reading, cooking, grooming).
  • Orientation and mobility (O&M) training: Teaches safe navigation using canes or electronic travel aids.
  • Daily living skills: Adapting activities like cooking, using appliances, and managing money.
  • Emotional and psychological support: Vision loss is life-changing. Counseling helps patients cope and regain confidence.

üè• Community Optometry: Vision Care Where It‚Äôs Needed Most

Community optometry bridges the gap between clinical care and real-life support. It ensures accessible, affordable eye care‚Äîespecially for underserved populations.

Roles of Community Optometrists in Low Vision Care

  • Early detection and referral: Identifying signs of vision loss early and guiding patients to appropriate resources.
  • Dispensing low vision aids: Fitting and training patients on proper use.
  • Education and awareness: Promoting eye health and low vision awareness in the community.
  • Home visits and outreach clinics: Especially crucial for patients with mobility challenges.
  • Collaboration with NGOs and government services: Connecting patients with rehabilitation programs, disability certificates, and social benefits.

üëµ Who Benefits from Low Vision Services?

Anyone with permanent visual impairment affecting daily functioning can benefit. This includes:

  • Elderly patients with ARMD or glaucoma
  • Children with congenital visual conditions
  • Patients with multiple disabilities
  • People recovering from vision loss post-surgery or trauma
